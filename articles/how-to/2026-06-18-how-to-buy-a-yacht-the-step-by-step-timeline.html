How to Buy a Yacht: The Step-by-Step Timeline

By Clark Haley · June 18, 2026 · 5 min read

Most first-time buyers ask me the same question early on: how long does this actually take, and what happens between the day I find the boat and the day I run it home? It is a fair question. A yacht purchase is not a car purchase, and it is not a house closing either. It has its own rhythm. Once you understand the steps, the process stops feeling like a black box and starts feeling like a plan.

Here is how a typical deal moves, stage by stage, with realistic timeframes and the spots where deals tend to bog down.

Step 1: Offer and acceptance

It starts with a written offer on a standard purchase and sale agreement. The offer names your price, the deposit, the contingencies (survey, sea trial, financing if applicable), and the dates by which each has to happen. This is not a handshake. It is the document the whole deal hangs on, so the terms matter as much as the number.

Acceptance can happen the same day or take a few rounds of back-and-forth. On a well-priced boat with motivated parties, you can have a signed agreement in 24 to 72 hours. The thing that keeps this stage clean is a broker who writes the offer tightly the first time, so you are negotiating price, not chasing vague language.

Step 2: Deposit and escrow

Once both sides sign, you put down a good-faith deposit, typically around 10 percent, held in escrow. The deposit shows you are serious and it takes the boat off the market while you do your due diligence. It is refundable if you reject the vessel during the contingency period for a legitimate reason, which is exactly why those contingencies are in the agreement. This step is quick, usually a day or two to wire funds into escrow.

Step 3: Survey, sea trial, and haul-out

This is the heart of the deal and the part you should never rush. You hire an independent surveyor who works for you, not the seller. Your Broker should provide you with a list of surveyors for you to interview and select the one that you think will give you the best level of service. Most buyers will want to hire a hull surveyor and an engine surveyor. On larger yachts, there may be additional surveyors that specialize in areas such as electrical systems, electronics, etc.The sea trial puts the boat under power so you and the surveyor can watch how she handles, how the engines load and hold temperature, and how the systems behave at cruising RPM. The haul-out gets her out of the water to inspect the hull, running gear, and everything below the waterline. 

"The survey is not a formality you check off before closing. It is the part of the deal that tells you exactly what you are buying."Clark Haley, One Water Yacht Group

Scheduling is what drives the timeline here. Lining up a surveyor, a haul-out slot at a yard, and a sea trial window that works for everyone usually lands this stage one to three weeks out from acceptance. Down here in Florida, yard availability in season can be the long pole, so we have to book early.

Step 4: Negotiating the findings

Almost every used boat comes back with findings. That is normal. What matters is sorting them: safety items, real costs you will carry, and ordinary wear that comes with a used boat. With that list in hand you have options. Ask the seller to make repairs, ask for a price adjustment that reflects the work, or accept the boat as is with full knowledge of what you are taking on. This is where a survey turns into leverage, and where a good broker earns the fee. Allow a few days to a week to settle it.

Step 5: Financing and documentation

If you are financing, your lender runs in parallel with the survey so you are not adding weeks at the end. Marine lenders want the survey, the purchase agreement, your financials, proof of insurance, and vessel documentation. If you are paying cash, this stage is mostly paperwork: title, registration or Coast Guard documentation, and any state sales or use tax handling. Plan on one to three weeks depending on the lender and the complexity of the documentation.

Step 6: Closing and delivery

At closing, funds move from escrow to the seller, title transfers, and the boat is yours. Then comes delivery, which is its own small project: getting the boat to your home dock, sorting insurance binding, and walking through systems so you actually know your new vessel. Closing itself is fast once everything lines up, often a single day.

The realistic total, and where deals stall

Start to finish, a clean deal commonly runs 20 to 45 days from accepted offer to keys in hand. Cash and good scheduling pull it toward the short end. Financing, a busy yard, or a complicated documentation situation push it longer.

Where do deals stall? Almost always in two gaps: waiting on survey and haul-out scheduling, and waiting on paperwork between accepted offer and closing. Both are avoidable with a broker who is working the calendar ahead of time and keeping every party on a deadline. That coordination, more than anything, is what keeps a good deal from dying of slow motion.
