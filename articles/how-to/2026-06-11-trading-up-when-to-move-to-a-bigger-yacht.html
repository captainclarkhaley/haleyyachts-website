Trading Up: When to Move to a Bigger Yacht

By Clark Haley · June 11, 2026 · 5 min read

Almost nobody buys their last boat first. You buy the boat that fits your life today, you run it hard, and a few seasons in you start noticing the ways it no longer fits. That is not a mistake. That is how this works. The question is not whether you will trade up. It is when, and how to do it without getting caught carrying two boats at once.

Here is how I help owners read the signs, time the move, and use a real valuation to plan it.

The signs you have outgrown your boat

Most owners feel it before they can name it. A few of the patterns I see again and again:

You are buying around the boat, not enjoying it. You skip the overnight because the berth is too tight for guests. You leave people on the dock because the cockpit is full. You pick the calm day because the boat pounds in a chop you used to shrug off. When the boat starts shrinking your plans instead of opening them up, that is the clearest signal there is.

Your range no longer matches your ambitions. A boat that was perfect for the sandbar and the ICW starts to feel small the first time you really want to cross to the Bahamas and stay a week. More waterline usually means more range, more comfort in a seaway, and a real place to sleep when you get there.

You are out of space for the way you actually use it. Kids got older. The guest list grew. You want a second stateroom, a bigger galley, a tender that does not fit the swim platform. Square footage on the water is the one thing you cannot add later.

The upkeep no longer matches the joy. Sometimes the boat is just tired, and the next round of maintenance is money you would rather put toward something newer and larger. That is a fine reason too.

Timing the sell-and-buy so you are not double-carrying

This is the part that worries owners most, and rightly so. Nobody wants to own two boats, two slips, and two insurance policies at the same time. There is no single right answer, but there is a right way to think about it.

"The goal is not to sell first or buy first. The goal is to control the overlap, so you are never paying for two boats with no plan for ending it."Clark Haley, One Water Yacht Group

In a market where good boats sell quickly, listing yours before you commit to the next one can leave you boatless for a stretch, which is its own kind of pain in season. In a slower market, buying first and then racing to sell can leave you double-carrying for months. The move is to run both tracks at once with your eyes open: get your boat listed and priced to move while you are actively shopping, and write your purchase offer with timing in mind so the two closings can be staged close together.

A few tools make this far less stressful. A bridge arrangement or contingent timing can line the closings up. A deposit and a tight contingency window on your purchase keep the new boat from slipping away while your sale finishes. And on the new-boat side, an order with a known delivery date, like a new build arriving on a set schedule, lets you plan your sale around a fixed point rather than guessing.

If the idea of buying and selling is too much to consider, or if you don't want to take a chance that you'll lose a season on the water with no boat or too many boats to manage, consider trading your old boat in with One Water Yacht Group. You may get slightly more if you list the boat, but the difference may not be significant enough to take a chance. 

Use a valuation to plan the whole move

You cannot plan a trade-up on a number you hope is true. Start with a real valuation of your current boat, based on what comparable boats are actually selling for right now, not what you paid and not what the market did two years ago. That number does three things: it sets a realistic asking price, it tells you how much equity you are bringing to the next boat, and it lets you build an honest budget for the upgrade including the carrying costs of the larger vessel.

From there the math gets clear. You know your trade equity, your financing or cash position, and the all-in cost of the bigger boat: dockage, insurance, fuel, and maintenance all scale with length. A trade-up that looks easy on the purchase price can surprise you on the annual carry, so we run both numbers before you fall in love with a transom.

Why doing this with one team helps

The cleanest trade-ups I have run are the ones where the same broker handles both sides. We list and market your current boat, we shop the next one, and we keep both timelines moving toward each other instead of leaving you to coordinate two separate deals. With One Water Yacht Group supporting me, I have the reach to sell your boat and the inventory and factory relationships to find your next one, including new Riviera builds with known delivery dates.
